Quick intro to distributed algorithms

We should develop adaptive notions to measure efficiency and complexity in parallel algorithms: work-depth.

The notion of “work-depth” relates to the dependencies between operations in an algorithm. We represent these dependencies as DAG’s (directed acyclic graphs).

Construction of DAG: Draw a directed arc from node u to node v if computation u is required as an input to computation v.

If the computation properly halts with an output, the DAG must be a tree.

Def[work]: T1=T_1=T1​= number of nodes in DAG

Note that the notion of work indicates the proportional time taken to execute the algorithm sequentially.

Def[Depth]: T∞=T_{\infty}=T∞​= depth of computation DAG

Smoothness and gradient descent

In gradient descent algorithms, we mainly study the proper step size. Decisions are made based on old information, i.e. gradients in old iterations. Smoothness assures that the old information is useful, i.e. the gradient does not change arbitrarily quickly so we can estimate the new gradient with the old ones.

Def[L-smooth]: A differentiable function f:Rn→Rf:\mathbb{R}^n\rightarrow\mathbb{R}f:Rn→R is said to be L-smooth if for all x,y∈Rnx,y\in\mathbb{R}^nx,y∈Rn, we have that

∣∣∇f(x)−∇f(y)∣∣2≤L∣∣x−y∣∣2||\nabla f(x)-\nabla f(y)||_2\leq L||x-y||_2∣∣∇f(x)−∇f(y)∣∣2​≤L∣∣x−y∣∣2​

Geometry of (strong) convexity

The article assumed that f(x,y)f(x,y)f(x,y) is μ\muμ-strongly-convex-μ\muμ-strongly-concave for μ>0\mu>0μ>0. That is, f(x,y)−μ2∣∣x∣∣2+μ2∣∣y∣∣2f(x,y)-\frac{\mu}{2}||x||^2+\frac{\mu}{2}||y||^2f(x,y)−2μ​∣∣x∣∣2+2μ​∣∣y∣∣2 is convex-concave.

What is the geometric intuition behind convexity assumption? Consider the definition of convexity, f(y)≥f(x)+⟨∇f(x),y−x⟩f(y)\geq f(x)+\langle\nabla f(x),y-x\ranglef(y)≥f(x)+⟨∇f(x),y−x⟩. This inequality implies the graph lies above the tangent plane.

What is the geometric intuition behind strong convexity assumption? Consider the definition of strong convexity, f(y)≥f(x)+⟨∇f(x),y−x⟩+μ2∣∣y−x∣∣22f(y)\geq f(x)+\langle\nabla f(x),y-x\rangle+\frac{\mu}{2}||y-x||^2_2f(y)≥f(x)+⟨∇f(x),y−x⟩+2μ​∣∣y−x∣∣22​. This inequality implies that the graph lies above the tangent plane and increases faster at least quadratically.

Strong convexity and first order methods

The paper we are talking about focused on first-order methods. What is a first-order method?

Problem class: any function that satisfies:

  1. dom f=Rn\text{dom }f=\mathbb{R}^ndom f=Rn
  2. fff is convex and differentiable
  3. ∇f(x)\nabla f(x)∇f(x) is L-Lipschitz continuous with respect to Euclidean norm and L>0L>0L>0, i.e. fff is L-smooth under the paper's definition.

  4. The optimal value is finite and attained at a certain valid point.

Black-box optimization & oracle complexity

Assume that the objective function is hidden in a black-box oracle and we can only access it through queries to the oracle.

In applications, this assumption occurs when the analytic form of the function is unknown, nasty, or we are using a surrogate model.

Def[Surrogate model]: A mathematical data-driven model that mimic the behavior of another model as closely as possible while being computationally cheap(er) to evaluate. (Wikipedia)

There are different types of oracles:

  1. 0th0^{\text{th}}0th-order: input x∈Xx\in\mathcal{X}x∈X, output f(x)f(x)f(x)
  2. 1st1^{\text{st}}1st-order: input x∈Xx\in\mathcal{X}x∈X, output the gradient ∇f(x)\nabla f(x)∇f(x) or a subgradient g∈∂f(x)g\in\partial f(x)g∈∂f(x)

Oracle complexity describes how many queries the algorithm make to reach a ϵ\epsilonϵ-optimal point.

Note that in real life implementations, one should consider the response time of the oracle. So oracle complexity only measures one aspect of the overall complexity.

Nesterov Accelerated Gradient and Momentum

Nesterov, 1983, proposed an accelerated gradient method, which usually takes this form: starting with y0=x0y_0=x_0y0​=x0​,

xk=yk−1−s∇f(yk−1)yk=xk+k−1k+2(xk−xk−1)\begin{aligned}&x_k=y_{k-1}-s\nabla f(y_{k-1})\\&y_k=x_k+\frac{k-1}{k+2}(x_k-x_{k-1})\end{aligned}​xk​=yk−1​−s∇f(yk−1​)yk​=xk​+k+2k−1​(xk​−xk−1​)​

Weijie Su, Stephen Boyd & Emmanuel J. Candès introduced a second-order ODE which is the exact limit of Nesterov’s scheme by taking small step sizes so that the trajectory or solution path converges to a curve. Here is THE ODE

X¨+3tX˙+∇f(X)=0\ddot{X}+\frac{3}{t}\dot{X}+\nabla f(X)=0X¨+t3​X˙+∇f(X)=0

with initial conditions X(0)=x0,X˙(0)=0X(0)=x_0,\dot{X}(0)=0X(0)=x0​,X˙(0)=0 and X˙=dXdt\dot{X}=\frac{dX}{dt}X˙=dtdX​.

First generalization of the Nesterov ODE: this equation looks like a damping system

for which, the Newton’s 2nd law motion equation is ma+cv+kx=0ma+cv+kx=0ma+cv+kx=0, i.e.

mx¨+cx˙+kx=0m\ddot{x}+c\dot{x}+kx=0mx¨+cx˙+kx=0

Here ccc is the constant of damping force. Look back at the Nesterov ODE. In the beginning, ttt is rather small so the analogous damping constant 3t\frac{3}{t}t3​ is huge (overdamping); as ttt increases, the damping constant becomes smaller (underdamping).

This transition from an overdamped system to an underdamped system fits the oscillation behavior of Nestrov’s scheme: the trajectory is smooth at first but it starts oscillating as the algorithm iterates more and more.

[Su, et al. JMLR'16]

The observation that the system of Nestrov ODE becomes underdamped, i.e. lack of friction, suggests that there will be unnecessary overshooting and oscillation along the ending trajectory.

So we can improve Nestrov’s scheme by introducing a speed restarting scheme. This is motivated by trying a different velocity coefficient to avoid overshooting.

Second generalization of the Nesterov ODE: we replace the concrete constant “3” with a variable rrr

X¨+rtX˙+∇f(X)=0\ddot{X}+\frac{r}{t}\dot{X}+\nabla f(X)=0X¨+tr​X˙+∇f(X)=0

Variational inequality

Equilibrium is a central concept in multi-agent learning and other disciplines like economics.

People have developed numerous methods to study equilibria, including (1) systems of equations, (2) optimization theory, (3) complementarity theory, (4) fixed point theory.

Variational inequality (VI) theory may unify these different approaches. It originates from mechanics.

Def[Variational inequality]: Given a mapping F:K⊂Rn→RnF:K\subset\mathbb{R}^n\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^nF:K⊂Rn→Rn, the variational inequality, denoted VI(K,F)\text{VI}(K, F)VI(K,F), is to find a vector x∈Kx \in Kx∈K such that

∀y∈K,⟨y−x,F(x)⟩≥0\forall y\in K,\langle y-x,F(x)\rangle\geq0∀y∈K,⟨y−x,F(x)⟩≥0
The set of solutions to this problem is denoted SOL(K,F)\text{SOL}(K, F)SOL(K,F).

We usually assume KKK to be closed and FFF to be continuous.

The geometric intuition lies in the notion of normal cone:

[Francisco Facchinei & Jong-Shi Pang. 2003]
