I encountered this interesting index trick in reading MIT 18.952 lecture notes.

Notation

First we introduce a notation

(−1)σ=∏i<jxσ(i)−xσ(j)xi−xj(-1)^{\sigma}=\prod_{i < j}\frac{x_{\sigma(i)}-x_{\sigma(j)}}{x_i-x_j}(−1)σ=∏i<j​xi​−xj​xσ(i)​−xσ(j)​​

where σ∈Sk\sigma\in S_kσ∈Sk​ is a permutation. This notation is valid as the value of RHS turns out to be either 111 or −1-1−1.

Why? Let p=σ(i),q=σ(j)p=\sigma(i),q=\sigma(j)p=σ(i),q=σ(j).

If we remove the restriction i<ji < ji<j and consider ∏i<jxσ(i)−xσ(j)xi−xj\prod_{i < j}\frac{x_{\sigma(i)}-x_{\sigma(j)}}{x_i-x_j}∏i<j​xi​−xj​xσ(i)​−xσ(j)​​, then any 2-tuple (p,q)(p,q)(p,q) and the corresponding term xp−xqx_p-x_qxp​−xq​ occurs only once in the numerator.

When we take the constraint i<ji < ji<j, the remaining term in the numerator is either xp−xqx_p-x_qxp​−xq​ or xq−xpx_q-x_pxq​−xp​.

So we conclude that the numerator and denominator are identical up to sign in (−1)σ=∏i<jxσ(i)−xσ(j)xi−xj(-1)^{\sigma}=\prod_{i < j}\frac{x_{\sigma(i)}-x_{\sigma(j)}}{x_i-x_j}(−1)σ=∏i<j​xi​−xj​xσ(i)​−xσ(j)​​.

Remark: the most important thing in linear algebra is that you have to understand the core idea instead of manipulating messy indices.

Homomorphism

Second we show that

σ↦(−1)σ=∏i<jxσ(i)−xσ(j)xi−xj\sigma\mapsto(-1)^{\sigma}=\prod_{i < j}\frac{x_{\sigma(i)}-x_{\sigma(j)}}{x_i-x_j}σ↦(−1)σ=∏i<j​xi​−xj​xσ(i)​−xσ(j)​​

defines a homomorphism. That is,

∀σ,τ,(−1)στ=(−1)σ(−1)τ\forall \sigma,\tau,(-1)^{\sigma\tau}=(-1)^{\sigma}(-1)^{\tau}∀σ,τ,(−1)στ=(−1)σ(−1)τ

which is the unique group homomorphism Sk→±1S_k\rightarrow{\pm 1}Sk​→±1

Alternation notation of k-tensor

The definition of alternation in the lecture note is misleading. Readers should be aware of the hierarchy inside the simulation provided by a permutation sign.

Everything is condensed in the following proof of Tστ=(Tσ)τT^{\sigma\tau}=(T^{\sigma})^{\tau}Tστ=(Tσ)τ:

Recall the definition

Tσ(v1,…,vk):=T(vσ−1(1),…,vσ−1(k))T^{\sigma}(v_1,\dots,v_k):=T(v_{\sigma^{-1}(1)},\dots,v_{\sigma^{-1}(k)})Tσ(v1​,…,vk​):=T(vσ−1(1)​,…,vσ−1(k)​)

So by this definition we have

(Tσ)τ(v1,…,vk):=Tσ(vτ−1(1),…,vτ−1(k))(T^{\sigma})^{\tau}(v_1,\dots,v_k):=T^{\sigma}(v_{\tau^{-1}(1)},\dots,v_{\tau^{-1}(k)})(Tσ)τ(v1​,…,vk​):=Tσ(vτ−1(1)​,…,vτ−1(k)​)

Now denote wi=vτ−1(i)w_i=v_{\tau^{-1}(i)}wi​=vτ−1(i)​, then

(Tσ)τ(v1,…,vk)=Tσ(w1,…,wk)=T(wσ−1(1),…,wσ−1(k))\begin{aligned}(T^{\sigma})^{\tau}(v_1,\dots,v_k)&=T^{\sigma}(w_1,\dots,w_k)\\&=T(w_{\sigma^{-1}(1)},\dots,w_{\sigma^{-1}(k)})\end{aligned}(Tσ)τ(v1​,…,vk​)​=Tσ(w1​,…,wk​)=T(wσ−1(1)​,…,wσ−1(k)​)​
