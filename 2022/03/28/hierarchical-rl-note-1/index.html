Hierarchy begins

Why do we need hierarchical algorithms?

Motivation: There are tasks with hierarchical structures. For example, people can decompose driving into three levels: 1) choose a destination; 2) turn/overtake/stop/park; 3) break/gas/steering. Look at this figure:

The following two are different:

  • “choosing actions from an oracle at a time step according to potential reward”
  • “switching the oracle/policy according to the current subtask”

This implies the two possible origins of complexity of a RL problem:

  1. Dimension of action space at time t (during one discrete time step)
  2. Number of heterogeneous subtasks/phases of the overall task (time intervals)

Semi-Markov decision process

To model tasks with hierarchical structures, we need the notion of semi-Markov processes. It was introduced in the context of continuous-time Markov processes.

Essential 1[Semi-Markov process]:

  • Set of states: S

  • Transition dynamics: P(s′,τ∣s)=P(s′∣s)P(τ∣s)P(s',\tau|s)=P(s'|s)P(\tau|s)P(s′,τ∣s)=P(s′∣s)P(τ∣s). τ\tauτ indicates the time to transition.

This process has two components: “jumping” state transition and “flowing” time dimension. The process “semi-Markovian” in the sense that only a semi part, the state transition, is Markovian.

Option framework

According to Doina Precup, “Artificial intelligence typically tackles large, complex problems. Solving such prob­lems requires adequate representations.” In hierarchical decision problems, we should generalize the representation of actions.

Essential 3[Markovian option]: A Markovian option is a 3-tuple, ω=(Iω,πω,βω)\omega=(\mathcal{I}_{\omega},\pi_{\omega},\beta_{\omega})ω=(Iω​,πω​,βω​)
