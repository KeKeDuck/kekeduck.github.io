Problem 1

(so Aj⊂Aj+1A_j\subset A_{j+1}Aj​⊂Aj+1​ for every jjj), then we have m(⋃j=1∞Aj)=lim⁡j→∞m(Aj)m(\bigcup_{j=1}^{\infty}A_j)=\lim\limits_{j\rightarrow\infty}m(A_j)m(⋃j=1∞​Aj​)=j→∞lim​m(Aj​).

Proof:

This is in essence a variant formula of the countable additivity of disjoint measurable sets. How can we construct an alternative expression, namely a disjoint union, for ⋃j=1∞Aj\bigcup_{j=1}^{\infty}A_j⋃j=1∞​Aj​?

It would be better if we visualize the ascending chain in a nesting venn diagram. Then the inspiration will naturally come to our mind.

Consider Bn:=An−An−1B_n:=A_{n} - A_{n-1}Bn​:=An​−An−1​

The rest of this proof is easy…

Proof:

One could naturally think of taking complements to turn this equality into the form of a union and then apply the result problem 1.

However, here we come to a point for a shift of perspective. We don’t just take complements in the space of Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn which will causes trouble as it introduces infinite measure.

Look at the condition m(A1)<+∞m(A_1)<+\inftym(A1​)<+∞. Instead we should take complements in the space of m1m_1m1​.
